# Illinois Municipal Governance — State Reference

> **Important**: This reference compiles Illinois statutory requirements, deadlines, and thresholds relevant to municipal governance. It is maintained as a research document, not legal advice. All statutory citations, dollar amounts, and procedural requirements should be verified against current Illinois Compiled Statutes and with qualified legal counsel before relying on them for compliance decisions.
>
> **Last reviewed**: March 2026
> **Covers legislation through**: SB 243 (effective January 1, 2026)

---

## 1. Constitutional Framework

### Home Rule (Article VII, §6)

- **Automatic home rule**: Municipalities with population **over 25,000** have home rule powers automatically (Article VII, §6(a))
- **Referendum adoption**: Municipalities under 25,000 may adopt home rule by referendum
- **Liberal construction**: Home rule powers are construed **liberally** (§6(m))
- Approximately **224** home rule municipalities out of ~1,294 total in Illinois
- **Key limitations** on home rule:
  - Cannot define or punish felonies
  - Cannot impose income-based taxes unless authorized by the General Assembly
  - Cannot incur ad valorem debt maturing beyond 40 years

### Dillon's Rule — Non-Home-Rule Municipalities (Article VII, §7)

Non-home-rule municipalities possess **only**:
1. Powers **expressly granted** by state law
2. Powers **incident** to those expressly granted
3. Powers **indispensable** to the declared purposes of the municipality

**This is the controlling legal framework for the target audience** (~15,000 population municipalities). Every action by a non-home-rule municipality must have identifiable statutory authority. Recommendations permissible for home rule municipalities may exceed a non-home-rule municipality's legal authority.

### Intergovernmental Authority (Article VII, §10)

- Authorizes units of local government to **"obtain or share services and to exercise, combine, or transfer any power or function, in any manner not prohibited by law"**
- **Intergovernmental Cooperation Act (5 ILCS 220)**: Implementing statute enabling joint action water agencies, waste agencies, emergency dispatch, sewage treatment, and other cooperative arrangements
- **Governmental Joint Purchasing Act (30 ILCS 525)**: Enables cooperative procurement across governmental units

## 2. Open Meetings Act (5 ILCS 120)

### Meeting Types and Notice Requirements

| Meeting Type | Notice Requirement | Agenda Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Regular** | Annual schedule posted at start of calendar/fiscal year; agenda posted **at least 48 hours** before meeting, continuously available | General agenda; final action on noticed items |
| **Special** | **48 hours** advance notice; same posting requirements | Discussion and action **limited to agenda items only** |
| **Emergency** | Notice **as soon as practicable** but prior to meeting | Bona fide emergency required |
| **Reconvened** | No new notice required if reconvening **within 24 hours**, announced at original meeting, agenda unchanged | Same as original meeting |
| **Schedule change** | **10 days' notice** by newspaper publication | Per changed schedule |

Notice posting locations: principal office, meeting location, and website (if maintained by full-time staff).

### Agenda Requirements (§2.02(c))

- Agenda must **"set forth the general subject matter of any resolution or ordinance that will be a subject of final action"**
- A body **cannot take final action on matters not on the 48-hour agenda** (PAC Opinion 14-003)
- Vague descriptions like "action as result of closed session discussion" are **insufficient** (PAC Op. 23-004)

### Closed Session

**Entry procedure**:
- Requires a **majority-of-quorum vote in open session**
- Motion must **cite specific exception numbers** from §2(c)

**No final action may be taken in closed session** — §2(e). This is an absolute prohibition in Illinois.

**Exceptions (§2(c))**: Approximately **39-40 categories**. Most commonly invoked by small municipalities:

| Exception | Section | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Employment matters | §2(c)(1) | Must relate to **specific employees**, not general budgetary matters or personnel policies (PAC Op. 15-007) |
| Collective negotiating | §2(c)(2) | Collective bargaining strategy and terms |
| Real property purchase/lease | §2(c)(5) | Acquisition or lease negotiations |
| Litigation — pending or probable/imminent | §2(c)(11) | Actual or imminent litigation |
| Tort immunity/insurance | §2(c)(12) | Self-insurance, risk management |

**Common compliance failure**: Using §2(c)(1) (employment) to discuss general budget or staffing matters that don't involve a specific employee. This is the most frequently cited OMA violation.

### Minutes Requirements

- **Open session minutes**: Approved within **30 days** or at the second subsequent regular meeting; posted online within **10 days of approval**
- **Closed session minutes**: Must be approved **in open session** (PAC Op. 23-014); reviewed **every six months** to determine if confidentiality remains necessary (§2.07(c)) — this review is mandatory, not optional
- **Closed session recordings**: **Verbatim audio or video recording** of all closed sessions is **mandatory** (effective 2020); retained for at least **18 months**

### Remote Attendance (§7)

Post-COVID rules (COVID emergency ended May 11, 2023):
- A **quorum must be physically present** — fully remote meetings no longer authorized absent a disaster declaration
- Remote participation requires the body to have **adopted rules** permitting it
- Qualifying reasons for remote attendance:
  - Personal illness or disability
  - Employment or body business
  - Family emergency
  - Unexpected childcare obligation (added 2023)
  - Active military duty (added January 1, 2026 via SB 243)

### Walking Quorum Doctrine (§1.02)

A **"meeting"** includes any gathering of a majority of a quorum discussing public business, including:
- Reply-all emails
- Text message chains
- Instant messages
- Social media group discussions

Established by **People v. Barr** (83 Ill. 2d 191). Serial communications that collectively involve a majority of a quorum discussing public business constitute a meeting subject to OMA.

### Enforcement and Penalties

- **Criminal**: Class C misdemeanor — up to **30 days jail**, **$1,500 fine**
- **Civil**: Mandamus, injunction, **voiding of actions** taken in violation, **attorney's fees** awarded to prevailing plaintiffs
- **PAC review**: Complaint must be filed within **60 days** (§8.1(e))

### Common Compliance Failures at Small Municipalities

Per AG/PAC (4,200+ complaints annually as of 2024):
- Improper closed sessions discussing general budgetary matters under the "specific employee" exception
- Taking final action in closed session
- Inadequate notice/agenda posting
- **Meeting rooms too small** — most common complaint (PAC Binding Op. 24-010)
- Walking quorum violations via email/text
- Failure to conduct semi-annual closed session minutes review

### Recent Amendments — SB 243 (Effective January 1, 2026)

Applies to both OMA and FOIA:
- **Active military duty** added as qualifying reason for remote attendance (OMA)
- **"Junk mail"** excluded from public records definition (FOIA)
- **Electronic requests** must be accepted in email body, not only attachments/links (FOIA)
- Bodies may **verify requesters are persons** — anti-bot provision (FOIA)
- New exemptions for criminal justice and nuclear security records (FOIA)
- **Website-only posting** permitted for §4 proactive disclosures (FOIA)
- **FOIA officer annual training** certification required (FOIA)

## 3. Freedom of Information Act (5 ILCS 140)

### Response Deadlines

| Request Type | Deadline | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Standard** | **5 business days** after receipt | §3(d) |
| **Extension** | Additional **5 business days** with written notice stating reason | §3(e) |
| **Commercial purpose** | **21 business days** | §3.1 |
| **Recurrent requester** | **21 business days** | §3.2 |

**Recurrent requester** definition: 50+ requests per year, 15+ in 30 days, or 7+ in 7 days.

**Failure to respond** within deadline = **deemed denial**; body then **cannot charge fees** for the request.

### Key Exemptions (§7)

| Exemption | Section | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Private information / SSN / biometric data | §7(1)(b) | Distinct from personal privacy |
| Personal privacy (balancing test) | §7(1)(c) | Public interest weighed against privacy invasion |
| Law enforcement records | §7(1)(d) | Investigation records |
| Trade secrets | §7(1)(e) | Proprietary business information |
| Deliberative process | §7(1)(f) | Predecisional communications only |
| Closed session minutes | §7(1)(l) | Until released per OMA §2.07(c) semi-annual review |
| Attorney-client privilege | §7(1)(m) | Legal advice communications |
| Collective negotiating records | §7(1)(p) | Except final agreements, which are public |

Exemptions are **narrowly construed**. Burden of proof on the public body by **clear and convincing evidence** (§1.2).

### FOIA Officer Requirements

- Every public body must **designate a FOIA officer** (§3.5)
- FOIA officer must complete **annual PAC electronic training**

### Fee Schedule

- First **50 pages** of black-and-white copies are **free**
- Additional pages capped at **$0.15/page** (B&W) or $0.25/page (color)
- Public bodies may charge up to 150% of cost for commercial purpose requests

### Common FOIA Compliance Failures

- Missing the 5-business-day deadline
- Inadequate denial letters (must include specific elements per §9: exemption cited, right of review, PAC contact information)
- Over-broad exemption application
- Failure to search personal devices and non-agency accounts for responsive records (§3(b) requires search of all locations where government business records may exist)

## 4. Governmental Ethics

### Statement of Economic Interest (5 ILCS 420/4A)

- **Filing deadline**: **May 1** annually, with county clerk
- **Who files**: Elected officeholders, appointed officials, certain employees
- **Required disclosures**:
  - Entities providing income **>$1,200**
  - Gifts **>$500**
  - Certain creditor relationships
- **CPI adjustment**: Dollar thresholds subject to adjustment every **five years** beginning 2025 (PA 102-662)
- **Late fees**: Begin May 1; increase to **$100/day from May 16**
- **Forfeiture**: Failure to file by **May 31** may result in **forfeiture of office** (§4A-107)
- **False statements**: Willfully false statements carry fines up to **$1,000** or imprisonment up to **one year**

### Gift Ban (5 ILCS 430/Article 10)

**Prohibited sources** — officers and employees cannot solicit or accept gifts from:
- Entities seeking official action from the body
- Entities doing business with the body
- Entities regulated by the body
- Entities with interests materially affected by the official's duties
- Registered lobbyists

**Key thresholds**:
- **Food/refreshments**: Not exceeding **$75 per person per day**
- **Cumulative annual limit**: Items totaling less than **$100 from any one prohibited source per calendar year**

**Key exemptions** (each mutually exclusive):
- Items available to the general public on the same terms
- Market-value purchases by the officer/employee
- Campaign contributions (governed separately under Election Code)
- Gifts from relatives
- Personal friendship gifts (not from prohibited source acting in official capacity)

### Local Ethics Ordinance Mandate (5 ILCS 430/Article 70)

- **Every municipality must** adopt an ordinance regulating political activities and gift acceptance
- Must be **no less restrictive** than the state Act
- Must include a **sexual harassment policy** (added 2017) and **independent review mechanism** (added 2019)
- This requirement is expressly a **denial and limitation of home rule powers** — no municipality may adopt less restrictive provisions, regardless of home rule status

### Additional Items

- **Ethics Commission/Advisor**: Not legally required but **strongly recommended** by the AG's Model Ethics Ordinance Guide. Without a commission, all complaints must go through costly judicial proceedings.
- **Revolving door provisions** (5 ILCS 430/5-45): Apply specifically to **state employees**, not directly to municipal employees by statute. Municipalities may adopt equivalent restrictions in local ordinances as a best practice.
- **ICMA Code of Ethics**: Contains 12 tenets (most recently amended May 2025). **Tenet 7 (political neutrality) does not apply to elected officials** — the Code primarily governs ICMA members who are appointed professional managers. Provides aspirational guidance for elected officials but is not directly binding.

## 5. Public Finance

### Property Tax Limitations — PTELL (35 ILCS 200/18-185 et seq.)

The Property Tax Extension Limitation Law limits year-to-year property tax **extension increases** (not rates or individual tax bills) for non-home-rule taxing districts.

- **Limit**: Lesser of **5% or the CPI increase**
- **Geographic scope**: Applies in **39 of 102** Illinois counties
- **Home rule exemption**: Home rule municipalities (population >25,000) are **exempt** from PTELL
- A ~15,000-population non-home-rule municipality in a PTELL county is subject to these limitations

### Truth in Taxation Act (35 ILCS 200/18-55 through 18-100)

- If the estimated aggregate levy exceeds **105% of the prior year's extension**, the district must **publish notice and hold a public hearing**
- Notice must appear **not more than 14 days nor less than 7 days** prior to the hearing
- **PA 103-1018** (August 2024): Website posting required for **30 consecutive days** for districts with staff-maintained websites

### Budget Systems

Illinois offers two systems under the Municipal Code:

| System | Statute | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **Appropriation Ordinance System** | 65 ILCS 5/8-2-1 through 8-2-9 | Passage within the **first quarter** of the fiscal year; public inspection at least **10 days** before hearing |
| **Optional Budget Officer System** | 65 ILCS 5/8-2-9.1 through 8-2-9.10 | Adoption **before the fiscal year begins** |

### Tax Levy

Tax levy ordinances must be adopted on or before the **last Tuesday in December**.

### Audit Requirements (65 ILCS 5/8-8-1 et seq.)

- Municipalities with population **≥1,000** (effective SFY 2027 per PA 104-0167) must file both an audit report and Annual Financial Report with the Illinois Office of Comptroller
- Audited financial statements must be posted on the municipality's website (PA 098-0738)

### Illinois Pension Landscape

| Fund | Funded Ratio | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| **IMRF** (non-uniform employees) | **~90-93%** | Well-funded due to **mandatory contributions**; model system |
| **Downstate Police Pensions** | **~55-60%** | Severely underfunded; local control of contributions |
| **Downstate Fire Pensions** | Significantly underfunded | Similar dynamics to police pensions |

- Statewide unfunded pension liabilities: approximately **$218 billion** (~49% average funded ratio — worst in the nation)
- **Statutory deadline**: Police and fire pension funds must reach **90% funded by 2040** — this will drive increasing budget pressure on small municipalities throughout the plugin's useful life
- **GASB 68** (pensions) and **GASB 75** (OPEB): Require recognition of net pension/OPEB liability on government-wide statements with Required Supplementary Information including 10-year trend data

### GFOA Best Practice Standards

- **Fund balance**: Minimum unrestricted general fund balance of **no less than two months** of regular general fund operating revenues or operating expenditures (~16-17%); many situations warrant "significantly" more. Must be formally adopted policy addressing conditions for use, replenishment plans, and prohibition on using excess fund balance for ongoing recurring expenditures.
- **GASB 54** fund balance categories: nonspendable, restricted, committed, assigned, unassigned
- **ADC**: GFOA's "Sustainable Funding Practices" advisory recommends contributing the **full actuarially determined contribution (ADC) each year** — under no circumstances engaging in contribution holidays
- **Pension obligation bonds**: GFOA **explicitly recommends against** these due to considerable investment risk
- **Debt ratios** to monitor: debt per capita, debt-to-EAV, debt service as percentage of general fund
- **Capital improvement plans**: At least **3 years, preferably 5+**; prioritize by health/safety; project future operating costs; evaluate affordability including impact on debt ratios and tax rates
- **Long-range financial plans**: **All governments** should prepare plans projecting revenues, expenses, and financial position a **minimum 5 years** into the future, reviewed annually

### 2026 GFOA Distinguished Budget Presentation Award

Major overhaul for 2026:
- New **points-based system** (>100 points for the award) replacing legacy mandatory criteria
- Evaluates content along **two axes**: information communicated and material type (documents, websites, dashboards, multimedia)
- Content categories: Community Priorities & Organizational Challenges, Value, and Long-Term Outlook
- Recognizes that "all governments/communities are different" — moves beyond prescriptive requirements

## 6. Zoning and Land Use

### Enabling Authority (65 ILCS 5/11-13-1)

- Enumerates **12 specific zoning powers** for municipalities
- **Subsection 11** explicitly authorizes the power to **"require creation and preservation of affordable housing, including the power to provide increased density or other zoning incentives"** — Illinois statutory authority for inclusionary zoning
- **Non-home-rule municipalities** derive zoning power **exclusively** from Division 13
- **Home rule municipalities** have broader authority from Article VII, §6(a) of the Illinois Constitution
- Municipalities may exercise **extraterritorial zoning** within **1.5 miles** beyond corporate limits under certain conditions

### Procedures and Deadlines

| Requirement | Statute | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| Public hearing notice | 65 ILCS 5/11-13-7 | Newspaper publication **at least 15 days** before hearing |
| Corporate authority action | 65 ILCS 5/11-13-7 | Must act within **90 days** after hearing conclusion |
| ZBA decision appeal | Administrative Review Law | Within **45 days** of decision |

### Legal Standards

**Special use permits vs. variances** — distinct legal tests:
- **Special uses**: Appropriate uses in a district that require **individual compatibility consideration**. The use itself is permitted; the question is whether it's compatible at the specific location.
- **Variances**: Deviations from specific dimensional or regulatory standards. Applicant must demonstrate **"unnecessary hardship"** or **"practical difficulty"**. Hardship must not be self-created.

**LaSalle/Sinclair factors** — the standard Illinois courts use to evaluate zoning decision reasonableness:
1. Character of the surrounding area
2. Property value impact on the subject property and surrounding properties
3. Public benefit from the proposed use or restriction
4. Comprehensive plan consistency
5. Gain to the public weighed against hardship on the property owner

Any ordinance analysis involving land use decisions should evaluate against these factors.

### Small Municipality Planning Context

- Many municipalities at ~15,000 population operate **without dedicated planning staff**
- Common staffing model: consulting planners, municipal attorneys handling zoning administration, regional planning commission technical assistance, volunteer plan commission members
- **CMAP** and other regional planning councils provide direct technical assistance
- Comprehensive plan costs typically range from **$25,000-$100,000+** using consultants
- **APA hybrid approach** recommended for small cities: form-based code for downtown/core areas, traditional zoning for peripheral areas
- **APA PAS Report 556** (*Smart Codes*): 16+ model ordinances including mixed use, live/work, affordable housing density bonus
- **APA Equity in Zoning Policy Guide** (December 2022): Recommendations on reducing minimum lot sizes, allowing greater density, permitting ADUs, reducing parking requirements

## 7. Procurement

### Competitive Bidding (65 ILCS 5/8-9-1)

- Non-home-rule municipalities must follow competitive bidding for public works exceeding **$25,000**
- Technology purchases are typically supply contracts with more municipal discretion
- Municipalities should establish procurement policies by ordinance (IML recommendation)

### Cooperative Purchasing

- **Governmental Joint Purchasing Act (30 ILCS 525)**: Enables cooperative procurement across governmental units
- Illinois state cooperative contracts available
- National programs: **NASPO ValuePoint**, **U.S. Communities** / **OMNIA Partners**
- Cooperative purchasing is the **most cost-effective procurement strategy** for municipalities at the ~15,000-population tier — reduces procurement overhead and leverages economies of scale
- **ICMA staffing model** for small municipalities: one IT generalist plus specialized contractors for complex tasks

## 8. Preemption Landscape

### Currently Preempted (State Law Overrides Local Authority)

| Area | Statute | Year |
|---|---|---|
| Firearms regulation | Firearm Concealed Carry Act | 2013 |
| Rent control | Rent Control Preemption Act, 50 ILCS 825 | 1997 |

### Not Preempted (Local Authority Preserved)

- Plastic bag and environmental regulation
- Short-term rental regulation

### Pending Preemption Threats (2026)

**Governor Pritzker's BUILD plan** — the most significant emerging preemption threat:
- **SB 4060-4064** and **HB 5626**
- Proposes sweeping preemption of local land use and zoning authority
- Includes: statewide zoning standards, mandatory ADU legalization, parking requirement limitations, impact fee formulas
- **IML vigorously opposes** these as preemption of traditional local authority
- IML's 2025 Legislative Principles expressly oppose preemption of traditional local rights, tax levy authority, land use decisions, and municipal employer authority; and oppose unfunded mandates

## 9. Institutional Resources

### Primary Resources for Elected Officials

- **Illinois Municipal League (IML)** — The primary resource for Illinois elected municipal officials
  - NEO (Newly Elected Officials) Workshops
  - *Illinois Municipal Review* publication
  - Fact sheets, model ordinances, and sample ordinance library (categorized by topic)
  - Annual conference
  - Legal assistance and Legal FAQs
  - Legislative advocacy and monitoring
  - IML Zoning Handbook for Municipal Officials
  - AG's Model Ethics Ordinance Guide (distributed by IML)
- **National League of Cities (NLC)** — National policy and research
  - *City Fiscal Conditions* annual survey (flagship benchmark data)
  - NLC University training
  - Policy position compilations
  - AI in Action initiative
- **GovAI Coalition** (300+ members) — Collective AI procurement standards; "Vendor AI FactSheet" requiring transparency disclosures

### Data and Benchmarking Sources

- **Illinois Comptroller's Local Government Warehouse** — Financial reports from **9,200+ local governments**; enables peer comparisons using Annual Financial Report data. The primary public source for Illinois municipal financial comparison data.
- **ICMA Open Access Benchmarking** — **80 free municipal KPIs** for self-service comparison. Note: ICMA primarily serves appointed professional managers; IML is the more appropriate resource for elected officials.
- **Census American Community Survey** — Demographic and socioeconomic data for all municipalities

### Planning and Technical Assistance

- **CMAP** (Chicago Metropolitan Agency for Planning) — Direct technical assistance to municipalities in the Chicago region
- **NIU Center for Governmental Studies** — Houses ILCMA secretariat; provides direct support to Illinois local governments
- **Regional planning commissions** — Technical assistance for zoning, land use, transportation planning

### Staffing Context for Target Audience (~15,000 Population)

- Staff typically wear multiple hats; dedicated specialists in IT, planning, and policy analysis are rare
- Most commonly outsourced functions: engineering, legal services, IT/managed services, planning and zoning, building inspection, financial auditing
- Typical operating budget: approximately **$15-25M**; IT spending ~2-5% ($300K-$1.25M)
- NLC reports local government employment declined by **300,000+ workers** between March 2020 and March 2022, intensifying staffing pressures
