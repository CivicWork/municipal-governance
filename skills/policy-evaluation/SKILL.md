# Policy Evaluation

## Overview

Effective policy evaluation provides decision-makers with objective analysis of policy options, their likely impacts, and trade-offs. Good policy analysis is evidence-based, considers multiple perspectives, and acknowledges uncertainty.

## Policy Analysis Framework

### The Eightfold Path (Bardach)
1. **Define the problem**: What exactly needs to be addressed?
2. **Assemble evidence**: What do we know about the problem?
3. **Construct alternatives**: What are the possible solutions?
4. **Select criteria**: How will we judge success?
5. **Project outcomes**: What will likely happen with each alternative?
6. **Confront trade-offs**: What are we giving up?
7. **Decide**: Recommend an option
8. **Tell your story**: Communicate clearly

### Problem Definition

**Good problem statements**:
- Focus on conditions, not solutions
- Are specific and measurable
- Identify who is affected
- Explain why it matters
- Avoid embedded solutions

**Bad problem statement**: "We need more police officers"
**Good problem statement**: "Response times to priority calls exceed 8 minutes in Districts 3 and 4, compared to citywide average of 5 minutes"

### Evidence Gathering

**Types of evidence**:
- Quantitative data (statistics, metrics)
- Qualitative data (interviews, observations)
- Expert opinion
- Peer community experience
- Academic research
- Historical trends

**Assessing evidence quality**:
- Source credibility
- Methodology rigor
- Relevance to local context
- Recency
- Potential bias

## Evaluation Criteria

### Common Criteria

**Effectiveness**: Does it solve the problem?
- Degree of problem addressed
- Speed of results
- Durability of solution

**Efficiency**: What does it cost?
- Direct costs
- Administrative costs
- Opportunity costs
- Cost per outcome

**Equity**: Who benefits and who bears costs?
- Geographic distribution
- Demographic impacts
- Ability to pay considerations
- Generational impacts

**Political Feasibility**: Can it be adopted?
- Council support
- Stakeholder acceptance
- Public opinion
- Legal constraints

**Administrative Feasibility**: Can it be implemented?
- Staff capacity
- Technical requirements
- Timeline realism
- Coordination needs

**Sustainability**: Is it durable?
- Ongoing funding
- Long-term viability
- Environmental impact
- Maintenance requirements

### Weighting Criteria
- Not all criteria are equally important
- Decision-makers should assign weights
- Trade-offs become explicit
- Facilitates discussion

## Logic Models

A logic model is a visual framework that maps how a program or policy is expected to work, from resources invested through results achieved. It is widely used in program evaluation, grant applications, and performance measurement.

### Framework: The Logic Chain

| Inputs | Activities | Outputs | Outcomes | Impact |
|--------|-----------|---------|----------|--------|
| Resources invested | What the program does | Direct products | Short/medium-term changes | Long-term systemic change |
| Funding, staff, equipment, authority | Services delivered, actions taken | Units of service, participants reached | Behavioral, condition, or status changes | Broad community-level results |

**Example — Youth Employment Program**:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Inputs** | $200K budget, 3 staff, employer partnerships |
| **Activities** | Job training workshops, resume coaching, employer matching |
| **Outputs** | 150 youth trained, 80 placed in internships |
| **Outcomes** | 60% retain employment at 6 months, improved job-readiness skills |
| **Impact** | Reduced youth unemployment rate in target neighborhoods |

### When to Use Logic Models
- **Program evaluation**: Map expected vs. actual results to identify where a program is working or breaking down
- **Grant applications**: Most federal and state grants require or strongly favor logic model submissions
- **Performance measurement**: Logic models identify which outputs and outcomes to track as KPIs
- **New program design**: Forces planners to articulate assumptions about how change will happen

### Relationship to Bardach's Eightfold Path
Bardach's framework helps choose WHICH policy or program to pursue by comparing alternatives against criteria. Logic models help plan and evaluate HOW a chosen policy is implemented.

Use them together:
1. **Bardach** (steps 1-7): Define the problem, generate alternatives, evaluate, and select a policy direction
2. **Logic model**: For the selected option, map the implementation chain from inputs through impact
3. **Bardach** (step 8, "Tell your story"): Use the logic model to communicate the theory of change to decision-makers

### Common Logic Model Pitfalls
- Confusing outputs (things produced) with outcomes (changes achieved)
- Assuming the chain is automatic — each link requires evidence that the connection holds
- Omitting external factors that influence whether outcomes materialize
- Building overly complex models that obscure the core theory of change

### Benchmarking
- Identify peer communities
- Define metrics
- Collect data

- Similar demographics
- Similar economy
- Geographic proximity
- Comparable services

## Risk Assessment

### Types of Risk
- Implementation risk
- Political risk
- Legal risk
- Financial risk
- Reputational risk
- Unintended consequences

### Risk Analysis Matrix

| Likelihood | Low Impact | Medium Impact | High Impact |
|------------|------------|---------------|-------------|
| High | Moderate | High | Critical |
| Medium | Low | Moderate | High |
| Low | Minimal | Low | Moderate |

### Risk Mitigation
- Avoid (eliminate the risk)
- Transfer (insurance, contracts)
- Mitigate (reduce probability or impact)
- Accept (acknowledge and monitor)

|----------|--------|----------|----------|----------|
| Effectiveness | 30% | 4 | 3 | 5 |

### Communicating Uncertainty
- Be explicit about what we don't know
- Present ranges rather than point estimates
- Identify key assumptions
- Describe sensitivity to changes
- Recommend monitoring approach

## Common Pitfalls

### Analysis Pitfalls
- Confirmation bias
- Anchoring on first option
- Ignoring opportunity costs
- Underestimating implementation challenges
- Overconfidence in predictions

### Process Pitfalls
- Insufficient stakeholder input
- Analysis paralysis
- Ignoring political reality
- Solution in search of problem
- Scope creep

## Quality Standards

- All claims should be sourced
- Options should be genuinely viable (not strawmen)
- Trade-offs should be presented fairly
- Analysis should be objective, not advocacy
- Uncertainty should be acknowledged

## Key Questions for Policy Analysis

1. What problem are we trying to solve?
2. What is the evidence base?
3. What are all the reasonable alternatives?
4. What criteria should we use to evaluate?
5. What are the likely outcomes of each option?
6. Who wins and who loses?
7. What are the trade-offs?
8. What are the risks?
9. Is it implementable?
10. How will we know if it works?
