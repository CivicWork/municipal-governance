# Council Communication

## Overview

Effective communication is essential for municipal governance. Different document types serve different purposes and follow established conventions. Understanding these conventions ensures clarity, professionalism, and legal compliance.

## Staff Reports

### Purpose
Staff reports provide council members with the information needed to make informed decisions. They should be comprehensive yet concise, objective yet include recommendations.

### Standard Format

```
STAFF REPORT

TO:         Mayor and City Council
FROM:       [Department Director Name], [Title]
DATE:       [Meeting Date]
SUBJECT:    [Clear, Specific Subject Line]

RECOMMENDATION
[Clear statement of requested action, suitable for motion]

SUMMARY
[2-3 paragraph overview of the issue and recommendation]

BACKGROUND
[Context, history, how we got here]

ANALYSIS
[Detailed examination of the issue]
[Options considered]
[Pros and cons]

FISCAL IMPACT
[Costs, funding source, budget implications]

ALTERNATIVES
[Other options council could consider]

ATTACHMENTS
[List of supporting documents]
```

### Writing Best Practices

**Recommendation Section**:
- Lead with the ask
- Make it motion-ready
- Be specific about the action
- Include ordinance/resolution numbers

**Summary Section**:
- Assume reader reads only this
- Cover the essential what, why, how
- Keep to 3-4 paragraphs maximum

**Analysis Section**:
- Present facts objectively
- Acknowledge counterarguments
- Support conclusions with evidence
- Be transparent about uncertainty

**Fiscal Impact**:
- Be specific about costs
- Identify funding source
- Note ongoing vs. one-time
- Include indirect costs

## Ordinances

### What is an Ordinance?
A local law of general and permanent application. Ordinances remain in effect until amended or repealed.

### Standard Ordinance Format

```
ORDINANCE NO. [YEAR]-[NUMBER]

AN ORDINANCE [BRIEF TITLE DESCRIBING PURPOSE]

WHEREAS, [recital of facts and findings]; and

WHEREAS, [additional recitals]; and

WHEREAS, [authority for action];

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT ORDAINED BY THE [CITY COUNCIL/BOARD]
OF THE [CITY/VILLAGE] OF [NAME], [STATE]:

SECTION 1.  [Findings incorporated or stated]

SECTION 2.  [Substantive provisions - what the law requires]

SECTION 3.  [Additional substantive provisions]

SECTION 4.  [Severability clause]

SECTION 5.  [Effective date]

PASSED by the [City Council] this ___ day of _______, 20__.

APPROVED:

_______________________
[Mayor Name], Mayor

ATTEST:

_______________________
[Clerk Name], City Clerk
```

### Ordinance Drafting Tips
- Each section should address one concept
- Use "shall" for mandatory requirements
- Use "may" for permissive authority
- Define terms consistently
- Cross-reference related code sections
- Include severability clause
- Specify effective date

## Resolutions

### What is a Resolution?
A formal expression of opinion, intention, or decision that typically addresses a specific situation rather than establishing ongoing policy.

### Common Uses
- Approving contracts
- Setting fees
- Policy statements
- Recognitions/commendations
- Grant applications
- Temporary regulations
- Procedural matters

### Standard Resolution Format

```
RESOLUTION NO. [YEAR]-[NUMBER]

A RESOLUTION [BRIEF TITLE]

WHEREAS, [background and justification]; and

WHEREAS, [additional recitals]; and

WHEREAS, [authority or purpose];

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE [CITY COUNCIL/BOARD]
OF THE [CITY/VILLAGE] OF [NAME], [STATE]:

SECTION 1.  [Statement of action or position]

SECTION 2.  [Additional provisions if needed]

SECTION 3.  This Resolution shall be effective [immediately/upon adoption].

PASSED AND ADOPTED this ___ day of _______, 20__.

_______________________
[Mayor Name], Mayor

ATTEST:

_______________________
[Clerk Name], City Clerk
```

## Proclamations

### Purpose
Ceremonial documents recognizing events, individuals, or causes. No legal effect but significant symbolic value.

### Standard Format

```
PROCLAMATION

WHEREAS, [background about the subject]; and

WHEREAS, [significance or achievements]; and

WHEREAS, [connection to the community]; and

WHEREAS, [additional relevant facts];

NOW, THEREFORE, I, [Mayor Name], Mayor of the [City/Village] of
[Name], do hereby proclaim [Date/Week/Month] as

[NAME OF PROCLAMATION]

in the [City/Village] of [Name] and urge all residents to
[appropriate call to action].

IN WITNESS WHEREOF, I have hereunto set my hand and caused the
Seal of the [City/Village] of [Name] to be affixed this ___ day
of _______, 20__.

_______________________
[Mayor Name], Mayor
```

## Council Memos

### Purpose
Less formal than staff reports, used for updates, FYI items, or matters not requiring action.

### Format

```
MEMORANDUM

TO:         Mayor and City Council
FROM:       [Name], [Title]
DATE:       [Date]
RE:         [Subject]

[Body of memo - more conversational than staff report]
[Can include bullet points for readability]
[Should still be professional and complete]

[Optional: "Please contact me if you have questions."]
```

## Constituent Correspondence

### Response Principles
- Acknowledge promptly
- Be respectful regardless of tone received
- Provide substantive response
- Explain "why" not just "what"
- Offer next steps when appropriate
- Follow up on commitments

### Standard Elements

**Acknowledgment**:
> Thank you for contacting us about [issue]. We appreciate you taking the time to share your concerns.

**Response to Concern**:
> [Substantive response addressing their specific points]
> [Explanation of policy/situation]
> [What, if anything, can be done]

**Next Steps**:
> [What will happen next]
> [How they can stay informed]
> [Who to contact for follow-up]

**Closing**:
> Thank you again for your engagement in local government. [We value your input / Please don't hesitate to reach out with additional questions.]

### Tone Guidelines
- Professional but warm
- Avoid bureaucratic jargon
- Explain acronyms
- Be honest about limitations
- Never be defensive
- Acknowledge frustration without validating inappropriate behavior

### Email Best Practices
- Clear subject line
- Lead with the answer
- Short paragraphs
- Respond within 48 hours
- Copy appropriate staff
- BCC for mass communications

## Minutes

### Purpose
Official record of meeting proceedings. Legal document that memorializes actions taken.

### Types
- **Action Minutes**: Brief record of actions taken (most common)
- **Summary Minutes**: More detail on discussion
- **Verbatim Minutes**: Word-for-word transcript

### Standard Elements
- Date, time, place
- Members present/absent
- Items discussed
- Motions (exact language of each motion)
- Vote tallies (yeas, nays, abstentions by name)
- Public comments (summary or verbatim, per local rules)
- Time of adjournment
- Clerk signature

### Level of Detail
- **Action minutes**: Record only motions, votes, and outcomes — most efficient, most common
- **Summary minutes**: Include key discussion points and rationale in addition to actions — useful for complex or controversial items
- **Verbatim transcript**: Word-for-word record of all proceedings — sometimes required for quasi-judicial hearings; labor-intensive

### Draft vs. Approved Minutes
- Minutes are considered **draft** until formally approved by a vote at a subsequent meeting
- Members review draft minutes before approval and may propose corrections
- **Contested minutes**: If a member disagrees with the draft, they propose specific corrections during the approval item; the body votes on each correction
- Once approved, minutes become the official record; they should not be altered except through a formal motion to amend previously approved minutes

### Closed Session Minutes
- Separate set of minutes kept for executive/closed sessions
- Access is restricted; typically reviewed periodically for possible public release
- Release timing varies by state Open Meetings Act — check `municipal.local.md` for local rules

### Retention
- Minutes are typically **permanent records** under state records retention schedules
- Both draft and approved versions may need to be retained, depending on jurisdiction
- Audio/video recordings may have separate (shorter) retention requirements

### Best Practices
- Record what happened, not opinions
- Use consistent format
- Note abstentions and recusals
- Reference attachment numbers
- Avoid editorializing
- Circulate draft for review

## Amendment and Redline Conventions

When presenting amendments to ordinances, resolutions, or other municipal documents, follow these conventions for clarity.

### Markup Standards
- **Strikethrough** for deletions, **underline** for additions — this is the most common municipal convention
- Present both a **redline version** (showing all changes) and a **clean version** (showing the final text) for council review
- Color coding is optional but helpful: red for deletions, blue for additions

### Section Renumbering
- When sections are added or removed, note explicitly whether remaining sections are renumbered
- If renumbered, include a table or note mapping old section numbers to new ones
- Some jurisdictions prefer to reserve the deleted section number (e.g., "Section 4. [Reserved]") to avoid renumbering

### Standard Clauses
- **Effective date clause**: State when the amendment takes effect — upon adoption, upon publication, or a specified future date
- **Severability clause**: Standard provision that if any section is held invalid by a court, the remaining sections remain in full force and effect
- Include both in every amending ordinance

### Codification
- After adoption, amendments are incorporated into the municipal code by the **codification service** (e.g., Municode, American Legal, General Code)
- The codification service updates the official code, applies section renumbering, and updates cross-references
- Allow lead time for codification — the official code may not reflect recent amendments immediately
